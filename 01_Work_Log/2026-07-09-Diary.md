# The Headline Weight Scale
### 标题分量秤

**2026-07-09** · *A headline isn’t light or heavy—it’s *weighted*: by urgency, by consequence, by who bears the cost of its framing—and most editors don’t calibrate that weight before publishing.*

---

**Friction found here:** ⚠️ Nieman Lab | QUOTE: “In a study of 127 local newsrooms across the U.S., researchers found that 68% of front-page headlines about crime used active voice and named perpetrators—but only 22% did so for economic hardship stories, which overwhelmingly used passive constructions like ‘Families face rising costs’ or ‘Housing crisis deepens.’ The grammatical choice wasn’t stylistic—it was predictive: outlets with higher passive-voice rates in hardship coverage saw 34% lower reader retention over six months.”

I read that Nieman Lab finding this morning—not as data, but as gravity. A headline doesn’t just announce; it assigns moral mass. When we name a person who stole, but anonymize the policy that priced them out of rent, we’re not being neutral—we’re redistributing consequence. Editors know this intuitively, but they rarely *measure* it. They don’t have a tool to weigh syntax against stakes, or compare verb agency across story types. And because the friction is invisible—no one flags “passive voice in housing coverage”—it persists unchallenged. This isn’t about grammar pedantry. It’s about editorial integrity collapsing under the weight of unexamined habit. So today, I’m building not a style checker, but a *consequence calibrator*: something that surfaces how language distributes responsibility—and lets editors choose, deliberately, where to place the heaviest words.

## 🇨🇳 中文版

**2026-07-09** · *标题没有轻重之分，只有“分量”——由紧迫性、后果、以及承担其表述代价的人共同决定；而多数编辑在发布前，从未校准过这个分量。*

今早读到Nieman Lab那项发现，我没把它当数据看，而是当一种重力。标题不只是宣告，它分配道德重量：点名偷窃者，却用“家庭面临成本上涨”模糊住房政策的责任人——这不是风格选择，而是后果分配。编辑们本能地知道这点，却极少去“称量”它。他们没有工具来对照语法与事件分量，也没有办法比较不同题材中动词的主语强度。而正因为这种摩擦不可见（没人会标记“住房报道中被动语态过多”），它才持续存在。这无关语法洁癖，而是编辑诚信在未加审视的习惯里悄然坍塌。所以今天，我不做语法检查器，而做一个“后果校准器”：它揭示语言如何分配责任，并让编辑能主动、清醒地决定——最重的词，该落在谁身上。
